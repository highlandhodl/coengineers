By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

- Transform written content into natural-sounding audio scripts

- Structure audio content for maximum engagement
- Write effective podcast intros, outros, and transitions
- Use Claude to iterate on script pacing and tone

**Say:**
"Welcome back! Today we're writing for the ear, not the eye.

Here's the thing about audio scripts: if you write them like articles, they'll sound robotic when read aloud. Audio needs rhythm, repetition, and a conversational flow. You're writing words that will be spoken, not read.

I've got an audio brief for you - someone wants to turn their written content into a podcast script. Your job is to make it sound like a natural conversation, not a lecture.

Ready to make some noise?"

### Step 3: Written vs. Spoken Language (5 minutes)

**Say:**
"Before we write, let's talk about what makes audio different from written content.

**Written language:**

- Complex sentences are fine
- Readers can re-read if confused
- Formal vocabulary is expected
- Visual structure (headings, bullets) aids comprehension

**Spoken language:**

- Short, punchy sentences work best
- Listeners can't rewind in real time
- Conversational vocabulary connects better
- Verbal signposts guide the listener

Here's an example. I'll show you the same idea written vs. spoken."

**Action:**
Present two versions of the same concept:

1. Written article style (formal, complex)
2. Audio script style (conversational, clear)

**Present it like this:**

```
## Written Version
[Formal paragraph with complex sentences]

## Audio Script Version
[Same content as conversational speech]

**Key differences:**
- [List what changed and why]
```

**Say:**
"Hear the difference? The audio version uses shorter sentences, contractions, and a rhythm that mimics natural speech. That's what we're going for."

**Check:** Wait for learner to acknowledge the difference

### Step 4: Crafting the Script Structure (8 minutes)

**Say:**
"Every great audio script has a clear structure. For podcasts and voiceovers, that's:

1. **The Hook** (0-15 seconds) - Grab attention immediately
2. **The Promise** (15-30 seconds) - Tell them what they'll learn
3. **The Content** (bulk of time) - Deliver on the promise
4. **The Recap** (30-60 seconds) - Reinforce key points
5. **The Call-to-Action** (15-30 seconds) - What to do next

Let me build out the full script from your brief."

**Action:**

### Step 5: Writing for Natural Delivery (7 minutes)

**Say:**
"Now let's talk about the art of natural delivery. The best audio scripts sound like you're having a conversation, not reading prepared remarks.

Here are the techniques:

**1. Use contractions:** 'You're' not 'you are', 'let's' not 'let us'
**2. Ask rhetorical questions:** Engages the listener's brain
**3. Use signpost phrases:** 'Here's the thing...', 'Now listen...'
**4. Vary sentence length:** Short sentences create rhythm. Longer sentences build detail and context, but you always return to short for impact.
**5. Include verbal pauses:** 'Well...', 'You know what?', 'Here's the truth...'

Let me take one section of our script and show you three different delivery styles."

**Action:**
Take one content section and rewrite it in three styles:

1. **Energetic/Enthusiastic** - For motivational or exciting content
2. **Calm/Authoritative** - For educational or instructional content
3. **Conversational/Friendly** - For interview or discussion format

**Say:**
"Which style fits the brief best? The tone changes the whole feeling of the content, doesn't it?"

**Check:** Wait for learner to choose their preferred style

**Say:**
"Excellent choice. Now let me revise the full script in that tone."

**Action:**
Regenerate key sections of the script in the chosen tone, showing how tone affects word choice, pacing, and structure.

**Check:** Wait for learner to review the revised script

## Common Questions & Answers

### Q: How do I calculate speaking time?

**A:** Average speaking pace is 150 words per minute for clear, conversational delivery. Slower (130 wpm) for instructional content, faster (170 wpm) for energetic content. Claude includes word counts in scripts for timing.

### Q: What if I'm writing for someone else's voice?

**A:** Include detailed speaker notes on tone, pacing, and emphasis. Consider their natural speaking style and write accordingly. Some people sound best with shorter sentences, others can handle more complex phrasing.

### Q: Should I write exactly what to say, or leave room for improvisation?

**A:** Depends on the format. Scripted voiceovers need word-for-word precision. Podcast discussions benefit from bullet points that allow natural conversation. Match the level of detail to the format.

### Q: How do I write for multiple speakers?

**A:** Clearly mark speaker transitions, write distinct voices for each person, and include dialogue direction. Think of it like writing a play script with stage directions.

### Q: Can Claude listen to audio and give feedback?

**A:** No, Claude can't analyse audio files in Claude Code. But Claude can review your written script and suggest improvements to flow, pacing, and naturalness.

### Q: What about adding humour to scripts?

**A:** Humour in audio is about timing and delivery. In scripts, set up the joke clearly, mark the punchline with (pause) cues, and trust the speaker to land it. Self-deprecating humour and observational comedy work better than elaborate setups.
